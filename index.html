Emissions Profile

Where Healthcare Emissions Actually Come From

Healthcare emissions follow the Greenhouse Gas Protocol classification. Most organisations measure Scope 1 and 2. Few can accurately quantify Scope 3 — yet it represents the largest decarbonisation opportunity.

Scope 1 — Direct Emissions

Emissions from sources directly owned or controlled by the organisation.

On-site natural gas combustion for heating and steam generation
Anaesthetic gases with up to 1,300× the global warming potential of CO₂
Fleet vehicles and patient/staff transport operations
Refrigeration systems and HVAC refrigerant leakage

Scope 2 — Indirect Energy

Emissions from purchased electricity, district heating, and steam systems. Typically 30–40% of total emissions.

Purchased electricity for clinical operations, imaging, and IT infrastructure
District heating and centralised steam systems
Water pumping, treatment, and distribution systems

Scope 2 typically represents 30–40% of a healthcare organisation's total carbon footprint.

Scope 3 — Supply Chain (≈80%)

The largest and most complex emissions category. Represents the greatest decarbonisation opportunity but is the least measured.

Pharmaceuticals contributing 15–20% of total emissions
Surgical instruments and medical consumables procurement
Food services, catering, and nutrition supply chains
Construction, maintenance materials, and capital equipment
Waste transportation, disposal, and treatment services
Laundry, cleaning, and external contractor services

Scope 3 represents approximately 80% of total healthcare emissions — and the largest decarbonisation opportunity.

Regulatory Landscape

Regulatory Pressure Is Intensifying

Sustainability in healthcare is mandated. Carbon management is now governance risk management.

2008
UK Climate Change Act
Legally binding carbon reduction obligations for all sectors including healthcare.
2020
NHS Net Zero Target
Net zero by 2040 for direct emissions and 2050 for the full supply chain.
2022
EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive
Mandatory ESG reporting and supply chain transparency requirements.
2023
WHO Sustainable Healthcare Guidance
Climate action and green procurement frameworks for health systems globally.
Ongoing
Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi)
More than 50 healthcare companies now validate emissions reduction targets against climate science.
Non-Compliance Risk Exposure
  • Loss of public contracts and tender eligibility

  • Restricted access to green finance instruments
  • Accreditation and certification risks
  • Reputational damage affecting recruitment and patient trust

Operational Strategy

Operational Pathways to Decarbonisation

Leading healthcare organisations act across multiple operational fronts. Transformation is achievable — but only when emissions are measurable.

Medical Waste Reduction

Structured segregation and alternative treatment technologies significantly reduce waste-related emissions.

30–40%infectious waste reduction via point-of-use segregation
80%emissions reduction via plasma gasification vs incineration
60%waste reduction with reusable surgical systems

Sustainable Procurement

Supply chain accounts for approximately 80% of total emissions. Procurement decisions are carbon decisions.

→Require science-based targets from pharmaceutical and device suppliers
→Prioritise regional sourcing to reduce transport emissions
→Apply lifecycle assessment instead of price-only decisions
→Source from ISO 14001 and B-Corp certified partners

Telemedicine Carbon Impact

Remote consultations reduce facility energy burden and eliminate patient travel emissions.

0.5–1 kgCO₂ saved per remote consultation
